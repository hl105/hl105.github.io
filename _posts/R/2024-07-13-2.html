sums of squares

  1. The Spock Example
    • Comparing more than two groups → need test statistic
    • Dr. Spock. Trial in Boston. 7 judges. Venires: sets of possible jurors for a case.
    • The sets of potential jurors "randomly chosen" for Spock's judge's trials include many fewer women than the set of potential jurors assigned to other judges
    • H_0: population means are equal across all of the groups (generalization of the null hypothesis for a two-sample t-test, that the two population means are equal.)
  2. What information should be included in our test statistic for comparing multiple groups?
    • A vs B: sample means same, A provides stronger evidence against H_0 because within group variance are smaller in A than in B, so pattern in sample means appears less likely to have occurred by chance → so need to estimate variance within group
    • B vs C: within group variances are the same, but sample means vary more in C. C provides stronger evidence against H_0 → variance in the sample means
    • A vs C: hard to telll! So we need a test statistic that will allow us to measure whether the sample means were likely to follow their particular pattern if the populations all had the same mean, given the within-group variances observed in that example.
  3. Equal means model (Residual model)
    • example: A: 8,10,18 mean(A) = 12, B: 2,4,6 means(B)=4 → overall mean = 8
    • $(8-8)^2+(10-8)^2+(18-8)^2+(2-8)^2+(4-8)^2+(6-8)^2=160$
    • How far the data points are from the overall mean
    • 160 = sum of squares total = SST = TSS = sum of squared residualsl = SSR
    • relates to sample variance: $s^2 = \frac{SST}{n-1}$ → $SST = s^2(n-1)$
    • reflect overall spread of output variable
  4. Separate means model (full model)
    • each group is a separate population with a different mean
    • $(8-12)^2+(10-12)^2+(18-12)^2+(2-4)^2+(4-4)^2+(6-4)^2=64$
    • 64=sum of squares within = SSW = sum of squared residuals, full = SSR, full = sum of squared errors = SSE
    • SSW is the numerator of pooled sample variance
    • $s_p^2 = \frac{SSW}{n-I}$ ← I = # of groups → $SSW = s^2(n-I)$
  5. SST and SSW
    • SST greater than or equal to SSW by definition
    • bc datapoints have a greater influence on their group than overall
    • by how much? if SST >> SSW → implies that population means likely different
    • we subtract overall mean from within group means
    • $(12-8)^2+(12-8)^2+(12-8)^2+(4-8)^2+(4-8)^2+(4-8)^2=96$
    • this 96 = 160-64 = SST - SSW = sum of squares between = SSB = sum of squares due to the model = SSM = Extra sum of squares = ESS
    • calculating how far the group means are from each other.
    • Sum of Squares Decomposition:
      • SST = SSW + SSB
      • SST: all the variation in the dataset
      • SSW: random variation within each group
      • SSB: the amount of variation in the dataset that is due to the difference between group means
  6. Extra sum of squares F-test
    • If sample size same in each group, $\frac{n}{I} = n_1$, the sample size of each group. Not requirement for this test, just a simplification for easer understanding
    • $SSB = n_1(group1mean - overallmean)^2 + n_1(group2mean - overallmean)^2 = n_1(sample variance of group sample means)(I-1)$
    • ESS F-test is a parametric test
      • generalization of pooled t-test
      • $H_0 = \mu_1 = \mu_2 = ... = \mu_I $
      • $H_A$: at least one population mean is different
      • assumptions:
        • normality within each group (robust)
        • independence within and between the group (crucial)
        • equal variance (not crucial if equal sample sizes)
      • $F = \frac{\frac{SSB}{I-1}}{\frac{SSW}{n-I}}$ ← test statistic. F is big when SSB is big

  • Q: Is there such a thing as a one-sided v. two-sided p-value for an F-test?
  • A: No. The F-distribution is not symmetric. Large values of F are evidence against the null, but small values of F suggest that the population means are quite similar. So, your p-value is always the probability that an F-distribution would be larger than your observed F-statistic.
